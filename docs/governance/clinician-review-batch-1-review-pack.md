### 1. Hyperthyroidism in Children

- Resource title: Hyperthyroidism in Children
- File path: `html-conditions/hyperthyroidism-in-children.html`
- Current status: `draft`
- Current medical_review_status: `pending-clinician-review`
- Last reviewed date: 2026-05-16
- Current references listed:
  - Indian Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Endocrinology. `https://ispae.org.in/`
  - Indian Academy of Pediatrics. `https://iapindia.org/`
  - NICE. `https://www.nice.org.uk/`
  - Pediatric Endocrine Society patient resources. `https://pedsendo.org/patient-resource/`
  - HealthyChildren.org. `https://www.healthychildren.org/`

Main parent-facing educational claims:

- Hyperthyroidism means too much thyroid hormone, making body systems work too fast.
- It may affect heart rate, weight, sleep, mood, growth, puberty, and school performance.
- Symptoms listed include weight loss despite appetite, fast heartbeat/palpitations/chest discomfort, tremor, heat intolerance, sweating, anxiety, irritability, sleep disturbance, school difficulty, eye prominence/irritation, and neck swelling/goitre.
- Hyperthyroidism can strain the heart and affect growth, puberty, mood, and learning.
- Symptoms may be mistaken for stress, behaviour problems, or exam pressure.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Urgent care for possible thyroid symptoms with chest pain, fainting, severe palpitations, or breathing difficulty.
- Urgent care for marked weakness, confusion, severe agitation, or drowsiness.
- Urgent care for fever with severe thyroid-like symptoms.
- Urgent care for rapid worsening, dehydration, persistent vomiting, or if the child looks very unwell.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Do not blame the child for restlessness, irritability, or poor concentration.
- Do not start or stop thyroid medicines without clinician advice.
- Seek prompt evaluation when symptoms suggest hyperthyroidism.

Any wording that may need clinician verification:

- "This can make body systems work too fast" and the listed effect on heart rate, weight, sleep, mood, growth, puberty, and school performance.
- "Hyperthyroidism can place strain on the heart."
- Behaviour/school-performance symptom framing.
- The urgency threshold for fever with severe thyroid-like symptoms and severe agitation/drowsiness.

Diagnostic, treatment, investigation, medication, or referral-related statements:

- A doctor may advise examination, pulse and blood pressure review, growth assessment, and thyroid blood tests.
- Further evaluation depends on the clinical picture.
- Do not start or stop thyroid medicines without clinician advice.
- The guide states it does not include antithyroid drug doses, treatment protocols, or follow-up schedules.

### 2. Hypothyroidism in Children

- Resource title: Hypothyroidism in Children
- File path: `html-conditions/hypothyroidism-in-children.html`
- Current status: `draft`
- Current medical_review_status: `pending-clinician-review`
- Last reviewed date: 2026-05-16

  - Indian Academy of Pediatrics. `https://iapindia.org/`
  - NICE. `https://www.nice.org.uk/`
  - Pediatric Endocrine Society patient resources. `https://pedsendo.org/patient-resource/`
  - HealthyChildren.org. `https://www.healthychildren.org/`

Main parent-facing educational claims:

- Hypothyroidism means too little thyroid hormone.
- Thyroid hormone is important for growth, brain development in early life, energy, bowel function, skin, temperature regulation, and puberty.
- Congenital hypothyroidism is present from birth, and newborn screening can help detect it early before symptoms are obvious.
- Acquired hypothyroidism develops later and may be related to autoimmune thyroid disease or other medical causes.
- Untreated hypothyroidism can affect height gain, puberty timing, energy, learning, and development.
- Impact depends on age, severity, and duration.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Seek medical review for poor feeding or prolonged jaundice in a baby.
- Seek review for severe sleepiness, poor growth, delayed puberty, marked constipation, neck swelling, worsening tiredness, or missed follow-up after an abnormal thyroid test.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Follow up promptly if a newborn screening result is abnormal or repeat testing is advised.
- If treatment is prescribed, give it exactly as advised by the clinician.
- Do not stop thyroid medicine because the child looks well unless the doctor advises it.
- Attend regular blood tests and growth monitoring when advised.

Any wording that may need clinician verification:

- Brain development and early-life wording.
- Congenital versus acquired hypothyroidism framing.
- "Newborn screening, where available" wording and follow-up threshold.
- Baby warning signs: poor feeding, prolonged jaundice, constipation, excessive sleepiness, and poor growth.
- Treatment and monitoring wording around regular blood tests and growth review.

Diagnostic, treatment, investigation, medication, or referral-related statements:

- Growth monitoring and blood tests are important when a doctor suspects thyroid disease.
- Children should be monitored by a clinician with blood tests and growth review when thyroid treatment is prescribed.
- If treatment is prescribed, give it exactly as advised.
- The guide does not include levothyroxine dosing or treatment schedules.

Suitability assessment:

- Source verification required.
- Clinician edit or explicit clinician approval required before marking reviewed.
- Not suitable for minor wording review only because it includes congenital/newborn screening and thyroid treatment follow-up language.

  - Pediatric Endocrine Society patient resources. `https://pedsendo.org/patient-resource/`

Main parent-facing educational claims:

- Late puberty is often a variation of normal development.
- Delayed puberty means expected puberty changes have not started by an age when most children have already begun developing.
- Puberty timing varies between children and families.
- Assessment considers growth pattern, nutrition, general health, family history, and emotional wellbeing.
- Constitutional delay of growth and puberty is described as a common pattern where a child develops later than peers but may otherwise be healthy.
- Broad causes listed include constitutional delay, undernutrition, chronic illness, endocrine causes, and primary gonadal causes.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Arrange medical review if puberty seems delayed or concerning.
- Review is especially important for poor growth, weight loss, chronic symptoms, headaches or visual symptoms, very low energy, delayed school progress, significant distress, or family history of endocrine or genetic conditions.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Avoid teasing, comparison with classmates, or comments that create shame.
- Offer privacy and calm explanations about body changes.
- Encourage balanced meals, sleep, activity, and follow-up appointments if advised.
- Tell the doctor about family puberty timing, growth records, medicines, chronic symptoms, and emotional concerns.

Any wording that may need clinician verification:

- "Late puberty is often a variation of normal development."

- Broad cause categories, especially primary gonadal causes and endocrine causes.

Diagnostic, treatment, investigation, medication, or referral-related statements:

- A clinician may review the child even when constitutional delay is likely.
- Assessment considers growth pattern, nutrition, general health, family history, and emotional wellbeing.
- Parents should report family timing, growth records, medicines, chronic symptoms, and emotional concerns.

Main parent-facing educational claims:

- Not every early body change means true puberty.
- Some children have isolated signs that do not progress, but rapid or very early changes need medical assessment.
- Early puberty means puberty-related body changes appear earlier than expected.
- Parents may notice breast development, testicular enlargement, pubic or underarm hair, body odour, acne, or rapid height spurt.
- The important distinction is whether changes are isolated/stable or progressive over time.
- Early puberty can affect final height if bones mature too quickly.
- Some children need assessment of bone age and growth pattern.
- Occasionally, early puberty may be linked with hormone or brain-related causes.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Prompt assessment for puberty signs at a very young age.
- Prompt assessment for rapid progression over months or sudden height spurt.
- Prompt assessment for headache, vomiting, visual symptoms, seizures, weakness, or other neurological symptoms.
- Prompt assessment for vaginal bleeding in a young child.
- Prompt assessment for significant behaviour distress, anxiety, bullying, or safeguarding concern.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Use calm, factual language and avoid blaming or shaming the child.
- Keep a note of when changes started and whether they are progressing.
- Seek assessment for rapid or very early changes.

Any wording that may need clinician verification:

- Neurological and safeguarding red flags.

Diagnostic, treatment, investigation, medication, or referral-related statements:

### 6. Short Stature in Children

- Resource title: Short Stature in Children
- File path: `html-conditions/short-stature-in-children.html`
- Current status: `published`
- Current medical_review_status: `pending-clinician-review`
- Last reviewed date: 2026-05-16
- Current references listed:
  - Indian Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Endocrinology parent resources. `https://ispae.org.in/`
  - Indian Academy of Pediatrics. `https://iapindia.org/`
  - Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne Kids Health Info. `https://www.rch.org.au/kidsinfo/`
  - Pediatric Endocrine Society patient resources. `https://pedsendo.org/patient-resource/`

Main parent-facing educational claims:

- Some healthy children are naturally shorter than others.
- Short stature means a child is much shorter than expected for age, sex, and family background.
- A single height measurement rarely tells the full story.
- Serial height measurements on a growth chart show whether the child is growing steadily, slowly, or crossing down height centiles.
- Some children are short because parents are shorter and they grow steadily along a lower centile.
- Some children grow later and enter puberty later, often with family history.
- Broad causes listed include familial short stature, constitutional delay, nutrition/chronic illness, endocrine causes, and genetic/syndromic causes.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Arrange assessment for height falling across growth-chart centiles.
- Arrange assessment for poor weight gain, weight loss, persistent tiredness, chronic diarrhoea, vomiting, cough, fever, or pain.
- Arrange assessment if the child is very short compared with family members.
- Arrange assessment for delayed puberty or puberty changes unusual for age.
- Arrange assessment for dysmorphic features, disproportionate body shape, developmental concerns, or learning difficulties.
- Arrange assessment for headache, vomiting, visual symptoms, or other neurological symptoms.
- Arrange assessment for history suggesting chronic disease, long-term steroid exposure, or major medical problems.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Bring previous measurements if available, including school records, vaccination cards, clinic notes, or old prescriptions with height and weight entries.
- Seek medical advice if worried about the child's growth.

Any wording that may need clinician verification:

- Definition of "much shorter than expected for age, sex, and family background."
- Growth-chart centile and crossing-down wording.
- Accurate measurement examples including shoes, posture, hair style, and measuring technique.
- Broad cause list, including endocrine, genetic/syndromic, chronic disease, and long-term steroid exposure.
- Red flags involving neurological symptoms and disproportionate body shape.

Diagnostic, treatment, investigation, medication, or referral-related statements:

- Diagnosis requires clinical assessment, accurate growth measurements over time, family height pattern review, examination, and selected tests if needed.
- Management depends on the cause and must be supervised by a qualified clinician.
- The guide does not provide treatment protocols or medicine doses.

Suitability assessment:

- Confirm common-cause list and whether any wording needs narrowing.

### 7. Types of Short Stature

- Resource title: Types of Short Stature
- File path: `html-conditions/types-of-short-stature.html`
- Current status: `published`
- Current medical_review_status: `pending-clinician-review`
- Last reviewed date: 2026-05-16
- Current references listed:
  - Indian Society for Pediatric and Adolescent Endocrinology. `https://ispae.org.in/`
  - Indian Academy of Pediatrics. `https://iapindia.org/`
  - Pediatric Endocrine Society patient resources. `https://pedsendo.org/patient-resource/`
  - Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne Kids Health Info. `https://www.rch.org.au/kidsinfo/`

Main parent-facing educational claims:

- Short stature is a description, not a diagnosis by itself.
- Many children who are short are healthy.
- Classification helps doctors think clearly about possible causes.
- Not every child needs many tests or treatment.
- Assessment is guided by growth chart, examination, family history, and general health.
- Broad types listed include familial short stature, constitutional delay of growth and puberty, nutritional/chronic disease-related growth faltering, endocrine-related short stature, genetic/syndromic short stature, and disproportionate short stature.
- The trend matters more than one measurement, and serial height/weight measurements help show whether growth velocity is appropriate.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Seek medical advice if growth is not following a steady pattern.
- Review is especially important if a child is falling across centiles, has poor weight gain, delayed puberty, chronic symptoms, unusual body proportions, or is much shorter than expected for the family.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Seek review if growth pattern is not steady or listed concerns are present.
- Use growth charts for pattern recognition rather than as labels.

Any wording that may need clinician verification:

- Category definitions for familial short stature, constitutional delay, chronic disease-related growth faltering, endocrine-related short stature, genetic/syndromic short stature, and disproportionate short stature.
- Statement that every child does not need many tests or treatment.
- Growth velocity and mid-parental height wording.
- Age-appropriate puberty-stage examination wording.
- "Basic investigations only if the clinical picture suggests they are needed."

Diagnostic, treatment, investigation, medication, or referral-related statements:

- Doctors assess by plotting height and weight on growth chart, comparing with mid-parental height and family history, reviewing growth velocity, checking puberty stage when needed, looking for chronic illness/nutritional/endocrine/genetic clues, and requesting basic investigations only if needed.
- Treatment depends entirely on cause and must be decided by a clinician.

Suitability assessment:
