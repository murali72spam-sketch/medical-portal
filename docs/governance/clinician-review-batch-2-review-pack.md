Resource title: Weaning Guide

File path: `html-conditions/complementary-feeding-after-6-months.html`

Current status: `published`

Current `medical_review_status`: `pending-clinician-review`

Last reviewed date: 2026-05-19

Current references listed:

- Indian Academy of Pediatrics parent nutrition guidance.
- ICMR-National Institute of Nutrition Dietary Guidelines for Indians 2024.
- World Health Organization complementary feeding guidance.
- HAB Eat behavioural feeding principles, limited in the page metadata to variety, repetition, persistence, no pressure, role modelling, and hunger/fullness cues.

Main parent-facing educational claims stated on the page:

- Weaning/complementary feeding is a gradual learning stage and is not a sudden stop to breastfeeding.
- Complementary feeding means adding safe, age-appropriate foods after the baby completes around 6 months while breast milk or formula continues as advised.
- Complementary foods are described as helping provide extra energy, iron, and nutrients as babies grow.
- Foods should progress from soft mashed foods to thicker, lumpier, and family-food textures as chewing and swallowing skills develop.
- Most generally healthy babies are described as ready around completion of 6 months, with readiness signs including good head control, sitting with support, interest in food, and spoon-feeding ability.
- The page advises against starting solids only because of night waking or curiosity before 6 months and directs parents to discuss early feeding needs with a paediatrician.
- Breastfeeding remains important for nutrition, comfort, and immunity during the weaning period.
- The page gives Indian first-food examples and emphasizes familiar family foods adapted without excess salt, sugar, chilli, or hard pieces.
- Responsive feeding is framed as encouraging without pressure, repeated exposure, hunger/fullness cue awareness, role modelling, and patient persistence.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Prematurity, poor growth, feeding difficulty, swallowing difficulty, neurological problems, complex medical problems, or another medical condition requiring an individual plan.
- Known or suspected food allergy, severe eczema, previous food reaction, family history of severe allergy/anaphylaxis, recurrent wheeze or asthma with food concerns.
- Poor growth, chronic vomiting or diarrhoea, blood in stool.
- Food reaction with facial swelling, breathing difficulty, repeated vomiting, lethargy, collapse, or concerning rash.
- Coughing, choking, blue colour, noisy breathing, or breathing difficulty during feeds.
- Persistent vomiting, severe diarrhoea, dehydration, blood in stool, or poor weight gain.
- Refusal of most feeds, marked feeding distress, lethargy, developmental regression, or repeated gagging with textures.
- Parent uncertainty about whether a food, texture, or feeding plan is safe.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Continue breastfeeding on demand, or formula as advised, while gradually increasing family foods.
- Start with soft, thick, freshly prepared foods that are easy to swallow.
- Use Indian family foods such as dal-rice, khichdi, curd rice, softened idli, mashed rice with vegetables, mashed vegetables, mashed fruits, dal, pulses, egg, fish, chicken, meat, paneer, or curd according to family practice and clinician advice.
- Add a small amount of suitable oil or ghee to home foods when advised.
- Offer variety, repeat safe foods patiently, avoid pressure-feeding, and watch hunger/fullness cues.
- Avoid honey before 1 year, choking hazards, tea, coffee, sugary drinks, packaged juices, cola, sweetened drinks, excess salt/sugar/chilli/fried snacks/ultra-processed foods, unsafe water, unhygienic foods, and cow's milk as the main drink before 1 year unless advised.
- Stop a concerning food and seek urgent medical review if severe allergy-type symptoms occur.

Nutrition quantities, age ranges, meal frequency, iron advice, food introduction advice, or feeding-behaviour advice that may need clinician verification:

- Starting complementary foods around completion of 6 months.
- Readiness signs listed for starting solids.
- "A few spoonfuls once or twice daily" around 6 months.
- Gradual build to regular small meals at 7-8 months.
- Movement toward family meal timings, safe soft finger foods, and thicker textures at 9-12 months.
- Modified family foods with snacks as needed after 12 months.
- Thick food rather than watery food.
- Protein foods including egg, fish, chicken, meat, paneer, or curd according to family practice and clinician advice.
- Oil/ghee advice when food is watery or low in energy.
- Texture progression and choking-hazard list.
- Allergy-risk exclusion wording and food-introduction boundaries.
- Behavioural feeding principles linked to repeated exposure, no pressure, role modelling, hunger/fullness cues, and persistence.

Diagnostic, treatment, medication, supplement, investigation, or referral-related statements:

- Parents are told to ask the paediatrician for an individual plan for prematurity, poor growth, feeding difficulty, or medical conditions.
- The page says to follow clinician advice for formula, supplements, or a special feeding plan.
- Allergy introduction, supplements, and special diets are stated to need individualization by the treating paediatrician when risk factors are present.
- Urgent medical review is advised for severe allergy-type reactions and feeding/breathing danger signs.

Main parent-facing educational claims stated on the page:

- Appetite often becomes variable after 1 year.
- Growth monitoring and food variety matter more than forcing large meals.
- Toddlers move from baby foods to family foods with suitable texture, safety, and supervision.
- Toddlers need regular meals, healthy snacks, water, and continued breastfeeding if the family chooses.
- Appetite can vary day to day, self-feeding interest is common, and mild food refusal can occur.
- Growth velocity slows after infancy, so appetite may look reduced.
- Too much milk, juice, frequent snacks, distractions, pressure, or grazing can reduce meal appetite or worsen mealtime stress.
- A skipped meal can be common; intake should be viewed over several days.
- Milk is useful but should not replace meals.
- Force-feeding can worsen refusal and mealtime stress.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Weight loss or no weight gain on serial measurements.
- Choking, recurrent coughing with feeds, or swallowing difficulty.
- Severe constipation, persistent diarrhoea, pallor, lethargy, or recurrent infections.

Home-care or parent-action advice stated on the page:

- Offer structure without pressure.
- Offer 3 meals and 1-2 planned healthy snacks adapted to family routine.
- Use family foods such as grains or millets, pulses, vegetables, fruits, curd or dairy, egg, fish, or meat where used.
- Offer nuts only in safe forms such as paste or powder.
- Give water as the main drink and avoid sugary drinks.
- Eat together and allow self-feeding under supervision.

Nutrition quantities, age ranges, meal frequency, iron advice, food introduction advice, or feeding-behaviour advice that may need clinician verification:

- Age range of 1 to 2 years for this guidance.
- 3 meals and 1-2 planned healthy snacks.
- Continued breastfeeding if the family chooses.
- Milk not replacing meals and too much milk reducing appetite.
- Water as main drink and avoidance of sugary drinks.
- Nuts only as paste or powder.
- Family-food list and self-feeding under supervision.
- Force-feeding and grazing cautions.

Diagnostic, treatment, medication, supplement, investigation, or referral-related statements:

- Medical review is advised for growth faltering, feeding/swallowing symptoms, severe constipation, persistent diarrhoea, pallor, lethargy, or recurrent infections.

Red flags / when to seek medical review stated on the page:

- Weight loss, poor growth, or falling away from expected growth pattern.
- Choking, coughing, gagging, breathing trouble, or repeated distress with feeds.

- Offer one familiar food plus one very small new or less preferred food.
- Eat together as a family when possible.
- Offer repeated neutral exposure, including seeing, smelling, touching, or licking food as progress.
- Plan meals and snacks so the child comes to meals with some appetite.
- Look at growth, energy, and variety over weeks, not one meal.
- Avoid force-feeding, punishment, threats, bribing, repeated pleading, screens, labels such as naughty/stubborn/difficult, grazing, frequent juice, or frequent milk between meals if appetite is reduced.
- For preschool/daycare, share a calm feeding plan, ask caregivers not to force-feed or shame the child, and ask them to report what was offered and roughly what was eaten.

## 7. Iron-rich Diet and Iron Deficiency Prevention

Resource title: Iron-rich Diet and Iron Deficiency Prevention

File path: `html-conditions/iron-rich-diet-iron-deficiency-prevention.html`

Current status: `published`

Current `medical_review_status`: `pending-clinician-review`

Last reviewed date: 2026-05-13

Current references listed:

- Indian Academy of Pediatrics parent nutrition guidance.
- ICMR-National Institute of Nutrition Dietary Guidelines for Indians 2024.
- Indian Pediatrics micronutrient deficiency prevention resources.
- Royal Children's Hospital Melbourne Kids Health Info nutrition and iron resources.

Main parent-facing educational claims stated on the page:

- Diet helps prevention.
- Confirmed anaemia often needs doctor-prescribed treatment, so food changes alone should not be relied on if symptoms are present.
- Iron is needed to make haemoglobin and support brain development, immunity, energy, and growth.
- Children can get iron from foods, but some need testing and prescribed treatment if deficiency is suspected.
- Possible signs of low iron include pallor, tiredness, irritability, reduced activity, pica, poor appetite, breathlessness on exertion, poor concentration, and sometimes few obvious symptoms.
- Possible reasons include low iron intake, excessive milk intake in toddlers, poor complementary feeding, rapid growth, menstruation in adolescent girls, worm infestation, chronic blood loss, and vegetarian diets needing planned iron-rich foods and vitamin C pairing.
- Combining iron foods with vitamin C foods can help absorption from plant-based foods.
- Jaggery alone is not a reliable treatment for iron deficiency anaemia.
- Iron medicines can be harmful in overdose and must be kept locked away.
- Iron supplement doses and duration should be decided by a clinician.
