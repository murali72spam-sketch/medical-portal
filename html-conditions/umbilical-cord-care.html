Umbilical Cord Care

Clean, dry, and watch for infection.

Parent Guide Draft

The cord usually separates on its own. Simple clean-and-dry care is usually enough. Redness, pus, bad smell, swelling, fever, or poor feeding needs medical review.

What is umbilical cord care?

Umbilical cord care is the routine care of the drying cord stump and belly button area after birth until it heals.

Expected changes

  • The cord stump becomes darker, drier, and smaller before falling off.
  • A small amount of dried blood or slight ooze around separation can occur.
  • The surrounding skin should not be spreading red, swollen, very tender, or foul smelling.

Home support

  • Wash hands before touching the cord area.
  • Keep the stump clean and dry.
  • Fold the nappy below the stump when possible to allow air drying and avoid rubbing.
  • Clean with plain water if soiled and dry gently.
  • Do not pull the stump off even if it looks loose.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Seek urgent medical review if any of these occur:
  • Bleeding, pus, spreading redness, warmth, swelling, increasing tenderness, or foul smell from the cord.
  • Fever or low temperature, poor feeding, refusal to feed, lethargy, irritability, floppy baby, or baby looks unwell.
  • Breathing difficulty, grunting, chest indrawing, blue lips, pauses in breathing, seizures, abnormal movements, or inconsolable crying.
  • Reduced urine output, dehydration signs, repeated forceful vomiting, green vomit, or abdominal distension.
  • Jaundice in the first 24 hours, deepening jaundice, poor feeding with jaundice, pale stools, or dark urine.
  • Persistent bleeding, a moist red lump after the stump falls, continued discharge, or concern in a premature or low-birth-weight baby.

Important facts for parents

  • Do not apply oil, turmeric, powder, ash, herbal paste, antiseptic, alcohol wipes, or home remedies unless medically advised.
  • Bathing advice may vary; the key is to keep the area clean and dry afterwards.
  • The stump should fall off naturally; pulling it can cause bleeding and infection.
  • Cord infection in a newborn can become serious quickly.

Medical disclaimer

General education only This guide does not diagnose cord infection, replace examination, or replace advice from your paediatrician. Seek urgent care for red-flag symptoms. Final clinical use requires clinician review.
