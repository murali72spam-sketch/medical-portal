Neonatal Danger Signs

When a newborn needs urgent care.

Parent Guide Draft

Senior Paediatrician & Paediatric Pulmonologist

Seek help early. In the first 28 days, babies can become seriously ill quickly. If a newborn has any danger sign, seek urgent medical care rather than waiting to see if it settles.

What are neonatal danger signs?

Neonatal danger signs are warning symptoms in a baby less than 28 days old that may indicate infection, breathing problems, dehydration, seizures, severe jaundice, low sugar, temperature instability, or another urgent illness. An online guide cannot diagnose the cause.

Danger signs to know

  • Poor feeding or unable to feed.
  • Very sleepy, limp, reduced movement, or difficult to wake.
  • Fast, noisy, or difficult breathing; chest indrawing; grunting; blue lips; pauses in breathing.
  • Fever, unusually cold body, seizures, or abnormal movements.
  • Jaundice in the first day of life, worsening jaundice, or jaundice with poor feeding.

What to do

  • Go to the nearest emergency service or paediatric hospital promptly.
  • Keep the baby warm during travel, but do not overheat.
  • Continue breastfeeding only if the baby is awake and able to feed safely.
  • Carry discharge papers, vaccination card, prescriptions, and recent reports.
  • Do not give unprescribed medicines, honey, herbal remedies, water, or animal milk while waiting.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Seek urgent medical review for any of these newborn danger signs:
  • Fever or low temperature in a newborn.
  • Poor feeding, refusal to feed, reduced urine output, dehydration signs, or baby looks very unwell.
  • Lethargy, unusual drowsiness, floppy baby, seizures, abnormal movements, or inconsolable crying.
  • Breathing difficulty, grunting, chest indrawing, blue lips, or pauses in breathing.
  • Jaundice in the first 24 hours, deepening jaundice, poor feeding with jaundice, pale stools, or dark urine.
  • Repeated forceful vomiting, green vomit, abdominal distension, blood in vomit or stool, or persistent diarrhoea.
  • Bleeding, pus, redness, swelling, or foul smell from the umbilical cord.
  • Parent instinct that the baby is seriously unwell.

Important facts for parents

  • Newborn fever is not treated like fever in older children; medical assessment is needed.
  • A newborn may be seriously ill even without loud crying.
  • Waiting overnight can be dangerous when a newborn has poor feeding, breathing difficulty, fever, or lethargy.
  • Early care saves lives; parents should never feel embarrassed for seeking urgent help.

Medical disclaimer

General education only This guide does not diagnose sepsis, jaundice severity, dehydration, hypoglycaemia, breathing problems, or any newborn illness. It does not replace medical consultation, examination, or treatment. Seek urgent care for danger signs. Final clinical use requires clinician review.
