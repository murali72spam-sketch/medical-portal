Bedwetting

Bedwetting is common and usually not the child's fault.

Children should not be punished or shamed for bedwetting. Kind support protects confidence while the family looks for triggers and gets review when needed.

What parents should know

Bedwetting means nighttime urine leakage in a child who is otherwise developing bladder control. It is common in school-age children and often improves with time.

This guide avoids medication advice. Persistent, new, or distressing bedwetting should be discussed with a healthcare professional.

What parents may notice

  • Wet bed at night.
  • Deep sleep and difficulty waking.
  • Embarrassment, hiding wet clothes, or avoiding sleepovers.
  • Sometimes constipation, daytime urgency, or daytime wetting.

What can contribute?

  • Delayed night-time bladder control, small functional bladder capacity, family tendency, constipation, heavy sleep, sleep-disordered breathing, stress, or excess evening fluids may contribute.

Practical home support

Keep the child on the same team. A practical routine helps without shame.
  • Reassure the child that bedwetting is not deliberate.
  • Use waterproof mattress protection and easy night clothing.
  • Encourage regular daytime toilet routine and relaxed toilet sitting.
  • Keep fluids spread through the day rather than loading in the evening.
  • Discuss constipation, bedwetting alarms, or other options with a clinician if persistent or distressing.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Seek medical review if any of these occur:
  • Pain passing urine, fever, blood in urine, recurrent UTI, new daytime symptoms, or constipation with pain.
  • Excessive thirst, weight loss, tiredness, snoring with disturbed sleep, or neurological symptoms.
  • New-onset wetting after being dry, significant distress, bullying, or safeguarding concern.

Important facts for parents

  • Bedwetting is not laziness or deliberate behaviour.
  • Repeated night waking may reduce wet beds that night but does not reliably train bladder control.
  • Medication decisions, if ever needed, should be clinician-led.
