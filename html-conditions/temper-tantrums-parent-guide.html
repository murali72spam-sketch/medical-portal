Temper Tantrums in Young Children

Tantrums are hard moments, not proof that a child is bad.

Parent Guide Published

Dr. Murali Gopal

Senior Paediatrician & Paediatric Pulmonologist

MBBS, DCH(UK), MRCPCH(UK), FRCPCH(UK), CCT Paediatrics (UK), Fellow in Paediatric Pulmonology (Aus), Allergology (Ind)

Young children are still learning self-control. Calm, consistent adults help children recover and learn safer ways to show big feelings.

What parents should know

Tantrums are intense episodes of crying, shouting, falling to the floor, refusing, or lashing out when a young child is overwhelmed. They are common during early childhood because language, patience, and emotional control are still developing.

Tantrums should be managed without shame, threats, or harsh punishment. The first priority is safety.

Common triggers

  • Tiredness, hunger, illness, overstimulation, or change in routine.
  • Frustration, communication difficulty, transitions, waiting, or being told no.
  • Wanting independence but not yet having the skills to manage it.
  • Stress at home, childcare, preschool, or school.

Practical home support

Stay calm and keep everyone safe. A child cannot learn much in the peak of a tantrum, but they can feel your steadiness.
  • Move sharp, hot, or unsafe objects away and stay nearby.
  • Use short, calm words and avoid long lectures during the tantrum.
  • Offer simple choices before difficult moments when possible.
  • Use predictable routines around meals, sleep, transitions, and screens.
  • Praise desired behaviour such as using words, waiting briefly, or calming down safely.

After the tantrum

  • Reconnect warmly once the child is calm.
  • Name feelings in simple language and practise what to do next time.
  • Keep reasonable limits consistent, especially around safety.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Speak to your child's doctor if tantrums are severe, unsafe, or linked with other concerns:
  • Self-injury, injury to others, dangerous running away, or frequent property destruction.
  • Loss of skills, developmental or language concerns, poor social interaction, or possible hearing concerns.
  • Trauma, abuse, bullying, family safety concerns, or severe persistent impairment at home or school.

Important facts for parents

  • Tantrums are not always deliberate misbehaviour.
  • Parents also need support; it is reasonable to ask for help if you feel overwhelmed.
  • This guide cannot diagnose autism, ADHD, anxiety, trauma, language delay, or behaviour disorders.
