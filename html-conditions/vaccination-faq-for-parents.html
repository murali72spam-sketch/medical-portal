Vaccination FAQ for Parents

Parent-friendly answers about vaccine safety, missed doses, mild illness, expected side effects, spacing, records, and when to contact a doctor.

Vaccines are used to prevent serious infections and reduce complications. This page answers common parent questions. It is not a full vaccine schedule, catch-up calculator, or substitute for a visit with your paediatrician using current IAP ACVIP, government, and local guidance.

What vaccines do

Vaccines train the immune system to recognize specific infections and respond faster if the child is exposed later. They do not promise complete protection, but they greatly reduce the risk of many serious infections, complications, hospitalization, and spread.

Some vaccines protect against diseases that can cause pneumonia, meningitis, paralysis, cancer, severe dehydration, brain infection, or death. The exact vaccine plan depends on age, previous doses, health conditions, risk, and current recommendations.

Why vaccination matters

Vaccination protects the child and also helps reduce spread in the community. This matters for newborn babies, pregnant women, elderly relatives, and people with weak immune systems who may be harmed more severely by infections.

In India, vaccine planning may include routine government vaccines and additional vaccines advised by the paediatrician. Timing can vary based on age, previous doses, health condition, outbreak or travel risk, vaccine availability, and current guidance.

Common parent questions

Should my child follow one fixed chart? Use the current schedule recommended for your child by the vaccinating doctor. This page does not publish a full schedule because guidance changes and catch-up decisions must be individualized.
  • Can multiple vaccines be given at one visit? Yes, multiple vaccines may be given at the same visit when recommended. This should follow current schedule guidance and the doctor assessment.
  • Are combination vaccines allowed? Combination vaccines can reduce the number of injections when appropriate and recommended for the child.
  • Should vaccines be skipped because of fear of fever? Do not skip vaccines only because fever may occur. Mild fever can be an expected short-term reaction after some vaccines. Discuss fever care with the vaccinating doctor.
  • Do vaccines have side effects? Like any medicine, vaccines can have side effects. Most are mild and short-lived, but serious reactions need urgent care.

Missed or delayed vaccines

If a dose is missed, parents usually should not restart the whole schedule unless the paediatrician specifically advises it. Contact your paediatrician, bring the written or digital vaccine record, and ask for a catch-up plan based on current guidance.

  • Bring all previous vaccine records, even if incomplete.
  • Tell the doctor about previous reactions, chronic illness, immune problems, medicines, travel, and outbreaks in the community.
  • Do not guess catch-up doses at home or copy another child schedule.

Vaccination during mild illness

Vaccines are usually not postponed for a minor cough, cold, mild diarrhoea, or low-grade fever if the child is otherwise well, but the vaccinating doctor should assess the child.

Vaccination may be delayed when a child is moderately or severely unwell, has high fever, has a serious allergic reaction history, or has a condition that affects vaccine eligibility. Ask the vaccinating doctor rather than deciding at home.

Common expected side effects

  • Mild fever, soreness, mild swelling, redness, tiredness, reduced appetite, or temporary fussiness can happen after some vaccines.
  • These expected symptoms usually settle with time and simple comfort care.
  • For mild expected symptoms, follow the advice given by the vaccinating doctor.
  • This page does not provide exact paracetamol or ibuprofen amounts.

When to call the doctor urgently after vaccination

Seek urgent medical care immediately for severe allergy-like symptoms or breathing difficulty. Watch for breathing difficulty, swelling of the face, lips, or tongue, widespread hives, collapse, fainting that does not rapidly recover, seizure, or a child who becomes very drowsy or difficult to wake.
  • Persistent inconsolable crying.
  • Very high or persistent fever.
  • Severe swelling, redness, pus, or worsening pain at the injection site.
  • Any reaction that makes the parent very worried.

Vaccine record and follow-up tips

  • Keep a written or digital vaccination record and take it to every vaccine visit.
  • Ask the clinic to write the vaccine name, date, batch details if recorded locally, and next due date.
  • Keep photos or digital copies of the vaccine card in case the paper card is misplaced.
  • Before travel, school admission, outbreak exposure, or a missed dose, review the record with your paediatrician.

Myths and facts

  • Myth: Vaccines have no side effects. Fact: Vaccines can have side effects, but most are mild and short-lived; serious reactions are rare and need prompt care.
  • Myth: A missed dose means the whole course must always restart. Fact: Many missed schedules can be caught up without restarting, but the paediatrician must plan this.
  • Myth: Natural infection is always better than vaccination. Fact: Many vaccine-preventable infections can cause severe disease, complications, or death.
  • Myth: Social media advice is enough for vaccine decisions. Fact: Use current reliable guidance and your child doctor, especially for catch-up or special medical situations.

Important safety notes

  • Do not use this FAQ as a complete schedule or catch-up calculator.
  • Do not skip vaccines because of fear of fever. Discuss expected reactions and fever care with the vaccinating doctor.
  • Tell the doctor about previous serious allergic reactions, immune problems, cancer treatment, long-term steroid or immunosuppressive medicines, pregnancy in adolescents, or severe illness on the day of vaccination.
  • Vaccine choices and timing may vary for premature babies, children with chronic illness, outbreak exposure, travel, or incomplete records.
