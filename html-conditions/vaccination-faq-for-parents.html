Vaccination FAQ for Parents

Clear answers to common parent questions about vaccines, timing, records, and aftercare.

Vaccination is one of the safest ways to prevent serious infections. Your child's vaccine plan should be confirmed with your paediatrician using the latest applicable IAP/ACVIP or national guidance.

Why are vaccines important?

Vaccines help the immune system recognise infections before a child is exposed in real life. They reduce the risk of severe disease, complications, hospitalisation, and spread to vulnerable people in the community.

Vaccination also helps protect babies, elderly relatives, pregnant women, and people with weak immune systems who may be at higher risk from infections.

How do vaccines protect communities?

When many people are vaccinated, infections have fewer chances to spread. This community protection is especially important for children who are too young for some vaccines or who cannot receive certain vaccines because of medical reasons.

What symptoms can happen after vaccination?

  • Mild fever, tiredness, irritability, or reduced appetite may occur after some vaccines.
  • Soreness, redness, or swelling at the injection site is common and usually settles.
  • Serious reactions are uncommon, but breathing difficulty, facial swelling, collapse, seizure, or a child who looks very unwell needs urgent medical attention.

Why does timing matter?

Vaccine timing is planned to protect children before they are most at risk. Delays can leave a child unprotected during the delay. At the same time, schedules may vary by country, vaccine availability, medical condition, and current guidance.

This should be checked against the latest official guidance at the time of consultation.

What should parents bring to a vaccine visit?

  • Vaccination card or digital record.
  • Previous hospital or clinic records if the card is incomplete.
  • Details of previous significant reactions after vaccination.
  • Current medicines and information about chronic illness, allergies, travel plans, or immune problems.

What if vaccines are missed or delayed?

Missed or delayed vaccines are common and can often be corrected safely. Do not ignore them, and do not assume everything must be restarted. The paediatrician should review age, previous doses, records, medical history, and current guidance before planning catch-up vaccination.

Respectful myth correction

  • Vaccines do not work by overloading the immune system.
  • Natural infection can be much riskier than vaccination.
  • Information from social media should be checked with reliable medical sources and your child's doctor.
