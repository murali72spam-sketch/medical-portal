🌡️ Fever in Children

A practical guide for parents on what fever means, how to keep a child comfortable, and when to seek urgent medical care.

Medical review status This parent guide is prepared for Dr. Murali Gopal’s Clinical Portal 2026 and should receive final clinical review before wider public use.
Reassurance for parents Fever is common in children and is usually caused by infections such as viral illnesses. The height of the temperature alone is not the only concern. How the child looks, behaves, breathes, drinks, passes urine, and responds over time is equally important.

1. What is fever?

Fever means the body temperature is higher than normal. In children, fever is commonly considered when the measured temperature is around 38°C or higher, depending on how and where the temperature is measured.

Fever itself is a symptom, not a diagnosis. It often happens when the body is responding to an infection. Many childhood fevers are due to viral infections and settle with supportive care, but some fevers need prompt medical assessment.

2. Symptoms and signs parents may notice

  • Child feels hot to touch or has a measured high temperature.
  • Tiredness, irritability, reduced play, body ache, or headache.
  • Reduced appetite but may still drink fluids.
  • Runny nose, cough, sore throat, vomiting, diarrhoea, rash, or ear pain depending on the cause.
  • Faster breathing or faster heart rate can occur with fever, but marked breathing difficulty is a warning sign.

3. Common causes

Fever can happen due to many causes. Common causes include:

  • Viral infections such as cold, flu-like illness, dengue-like viral illnesses, or gastroenteritis.
  • Throat, ear, chest, urinary, skin, or abdominal infections.
  • Fever after vaccination, which is usually short-lived but should still be monitored.
  • Less commonly, serious infections or inflammatory conditions requiring urgent care.

The exact cause cannot always be identified from temperature alone. Clinical assessment is needed when red flags are present or when the child is worsening.

4. Home management

Main aim at home Keep the child comfortable, hydrated, and closely observed. Do not focus only on “bringing the number down.”

Fluids and feeding

  • Offer frequent small amounts of fluids.
  • Breastfed babies should continue breastfeeding.
  • Do not force food. Appetite often improves when the illness settles.
  • Watch urine output. Passing much less urine than usual can suggest dehydration.

Comfort measures

  • Let the child rest in a comfortable, well-ventilated room.
  • Use light clothing. Avoid over-wrapping or heavy blankets.
  • Avoid cold baths, ice baths, and alcohol sponging.
  • Check the child during sleep if the fever is high or the child seems more unwell.

Medicines

Fever medicines may help if the child is uncomfortable, in pain, or unable to rest. Use only medicines appropriate for the child’s age and weight, and follow the dose advised by your paediatrician or the medicine label. This guide intentionally does not provide dose calculations.

Do not give aspirin to children unless specifically prescribed by a doctor.

5. Red flags — when to seek urgent medical help

Seek urgent medical care now if any of these are present:
  • Baby younger than 3 months with fever.
  • Difficulty breathing, grunting, chest indrawing, blue lips, or unusually fast breathing.
  • Child is very drowsy, confused, difficult to wake, floppy, or unusually weak.
  • Seizure, abnormal movements, or loss of consciousness.
  • Stiff neck, severe headache, persistent vomiting, or light sensitivity.
  • Non-blanching rash, purple spots, or rapidly spreading rash.
  • Signs of dehydration: very dry mouth, no tears, sunken eyes, or markedly reduced urine.
  • Persistent crying, inconsolability, severe pain, or worsening condition.
  • Fever in a child with significant underlying disease, immune suppression, or complex medical problems.
  • Fever that persists, recurs, or worries the parent despite home care.

6. Important facts and myth correction

  • Myth: Higher temperature always means serious disease.
    Fact: The child’s overall appearance and red flags matter greatly.
  • Myth: Fever must always be completely normalized.
    Fact: Comfort, hydration, and observation are more important than chasing a normal number.
  • Myth: Cold baths are a good way to reduce fever.
    Fact: Cold baths can cause shivering and discomfort and are not recommended.
  • Myth: Antibiotics are needed for every fever.
    Fact: Many childhood fevers are viral. Antibiotics are useful only when a bacterial infection is suspected or confirmed by a doctor.
  • Myth: Teething explains any high fever.
    Fact: Do not assume significant fever is due to teething without considering infection or other illness.

7. What to monitor at home

Observe Why it matters
Activity and alertness A playful, alert child is usually less concerning than a lethargic or confused child.
Breathing Breathing difficulty is more important than the temperature reading alone.
Fluid intake Poor intake increases dehydration risk.
Urine output Markedly reduced urine can indicate dehydration.
Rash, pain, vomiting, diarrhoea Associated symptoms help identify possible causes and urgency.
