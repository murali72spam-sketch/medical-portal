Normal Newborn Care

First weeks made safer, calmer, and more confident.

Parent Guide Draft

Simple care matters. Most newborn care is about warmth, feeding, safe sleep, clean hands, and early help if something feels wrong. In the first month, parents should contact their paediatrician even for small concerns.

What is normal newborn care?

Normal newborn care means the everyday care that helps a baby stay warm, feed well, sleep safely, avoid infection, and attend timely follow-up visits after birth.

What parents may notice

  • Newborns sleep for many hours but should wake for feeds.
  • Urine and stool patterns change over the first week.
  • Sneezing, hiccups, startles, brief irregular breathing, mild peeling skin, breast swelling, or small vaginal mucus/spotting can occur in some babies.
  • Regular feeding, jaundice observation, temperature awareness, and activity level are important.

Home support

  • Wash hands before handling the baby and before feeds.
  • Keep the baby comfortably warm, while avoiding overheating or tight wrapping.
  • Feed frequently and watch for active sucking, swallowing, urine output, and alert periods.
  • Use safe sleep for every sleep: baby on the back, on a firm flat surface, with no pillows or loose bedding.
  • Attend planned newborn check-ups, weight checks, jaundice checks, and immunisations as advised.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Seek urgent medical review if any of these occur:
  • Fever or unusually low temperature in a newborn.
  • Poor feeding, refusal to feed, repeated vomiting, or not waking for feeds.
  • Lethargy, unusual drowsiness, floppy baby, seizures, abnormal movements, or inconsolable crying.
  • Fast or difficult breathing, grunting, chest indrawing, blue lips, or pauses in breathing.
  • Jaundice in the first 24 hours, deepening jaundice, poor feeding with jaundice, pale stools, or dark urine.
  • Reduced urine output, sunken eyes, very dry mouth, abdominal distension, green vomit, or repeated forceful vomiting.
  • Bleeding, pus, redness, swelling, or foul smell from the umbilical cord.
  • Baby looks very unwell, or parents feel something is not right.

Important facts for parents

  • Do not give honey, gripe water, herbal mixtures, water, animal milk, or medicines unless prescribed.
  • Newborn sneezing is often normal and does not always mean a cold.
  • Visitors should wash hands and avoid close contact if they have cough, fever, cold sores, vomiting, or diarrhoea.
  • Responsive care does not spoil a baby; it helps babies feel safe.

Medical disclaimer
