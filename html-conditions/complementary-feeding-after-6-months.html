Complementary Feeding After 6 Months

Starting family foods safely while continuing breast milk or formula as advised.

Learning to eat is gradual. Babies often need time, repeated exposure, and calm support. Complementary feeding is not stopping breastfeeding; both continue together unless your clinician advises otherwise.

What is complementary feeding?

Complementary feeding means giving safe, age-appropriate family foods after completion of 6 months, while continuing breastfeeding or formula as advised. Foods should gradually become thicker, more varied, and more textured as the child grows.

Readiness and normal learning

  • Baby may show readiness by sitting with support, having good head control, and showing interest in food.
  • Start with small amounts and gradually increase variety.
  • Gagging can happen while learning; choking, blue colour, or breathing difficulty is dangerous.

Why complementary foods matter

  • After 6 months, breast milk alone is usually not enough for all energy, iron, and nutrient needs.
  • Very watery foods may fill the stomach without providing enough energy.
  • Unsafe feeding practices can increase choking, diarrhoea, or infection risk.

Home feeding approach

Use safe family foods. Keep textures soft and thick enough, feed patiently, and avoid force-feeding.
  • Start with thick, soft mashed foods such as dal-rice, khichdi, mashed vegetables, fruit, and family foods adapted safely.
  • Offer variety across grains, pulses, egg, meat, fish where culturally acceptable, dairy, fruits, and vegetables.
  • Use clean hands, clean utensils, and safe water.
  • Do not give honey to infants under 1 year.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Seek urgent care or prompt review if any of these occur:
  • Coughing or choking, blue colour, or breathing difficulty during feeding.
  • Persistent vomiting, severe diarrhoea, dehydration, or poor weight gain.
  • Refusal of most feeds, lethargy, developmental regression, or repeated feeding distress.

Important facts for parents

  • Responsive feeding means encouraging, not forcing.
  • Iron-rich foods are important, but supplement doses should be clinician-guided.
  • Each child progresses at a different pace; growth and safety matter more than a rigid meal plan.
