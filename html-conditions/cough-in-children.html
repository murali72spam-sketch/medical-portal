😷 Cough in Children

A practical guide for parents on common causes of cough, safe home care, and warning signs that need medical assessment.

Medical review status This parent guide is prepared for Dr. Murali Gopal’s Clinical Portal 2026 and should receive final clinical review before wider public use.
Reassurance for parents Cough is very common in children. Most short-duration coughs are due to viral upper respiratory infections and improve with time and supportive care. The important task is to watch the child’s breathing, hydration, activity, and warning signs rather than treating the cough sound alone.

1. What is cough?

Cough is a protective reflex that helps clear mucus, secretions, irritants, or infection-related inflammation from the airways. It is a symptom, not a diagnosis.

A cough can sound dry, wet, barking, spasmodic, or wheezy. The sound may give clues, but the child’s overall condition is more important than the sound alone.

2. Symptoms and signs parents may notice

  • Dry cough or wet cough with mucus sounds.
  • Runny nose, blocked nose, sneezing, sore throat, or fever.
  • Cough that is worse at night, after activity, or after lying down.
  • Hoarse voice or barking cough, which may suggest croup-like illness.
  • Whistling sound while breathing out, which may suggest wheeze.
  • Vomiting after repeated coughing bouts.
  • Reduced sleep, appetite, or play due to cough.

3. Common causes

Cough in children can have many causes. Common possibilities include:

  • Viral cold or upper respiratory infection.
  • Post-nasal drip from a blocked or runny nose.
  • Wheeze or asthma-like airway narrowing.
  • Croup, especially if the cough is barking and associated with noisy breathing.
  • Bronchiolitis in infants and younger children.
  • Pneumonia or chest infection, especially with fever, fast breathing, or worsening illness.
  • Allergy, irritants, smoke, pollution, dust, or strong smells.
  • Foreign body aspiration, especially if cough starts suddenly during eating or play.
  • Reflux or repeated throat irritation in selected children.

The exact cause may need clinical assessment, especially when the child is young, breathless, persistently febrile, worsening, or has recurrent cough.

4. Home management

Main aim at home Keep the child comfortable, hydrated, and breathing comfortably. Avoid unnecessary cough medicines unless advised by a doctor.

Fluids and comfort

  • Offer frequent small amounts of fluids.
  • Continue breastfeeding in breastfed infants.
  • Allow rest, but the child does not need strict bed rest if otherwise active.
  • Use a comfortable sleeping position. Do not use unsafe pillows or positioning in infants.

Nose care

  • A blocked nose can worsen cough, especially at night.
  • Saline nose drops or spray may help some children with nasal blockage.
  • Gentle nose cleaning may be useful in babies if feeding is affected.
  • Avoid forceful suction or repeated aggressive nose cleaning.

Honey

Honey may soothe cough in some children older than 1 year. Do not give honey to babies under 12 months because of the risk of infant botulism.

Avoid smoke and irritants

  • Keep the child away from cigarette smoke, incense smoke, strong perfumes, dust, and pollution where possible.
  • Do not smoke inside the house, car, balcony, or near the child.

Cough and cold medicines

Do not self-medicate children with cough syrups, sedating antihistamines, decongestants, bronchodilators, antibiotics, or leftover prescriptions. These medicines should be used only when appropriate and prescribed after clinical assessment.

This guide intentionally does not provide medicine doses or cough-syrup selection advice because treatment depends on the child’s age, weight, diagnosis, breathing status, and clinical examination.

5. Red flags — when to seek urgent medical help

Seek urgent medical care now if any of these are present:
  • Difficulty breathing, chest indrawing, grunting, or child struggling to breathe.
  • Blue lips, pale/grey appearance, or episodes of stopping breathing.
  • Noisy breathing at rest, especially harsh sound while breathing in.
  • Very fast breathing, severe wheeze, or inability to speak/feed due to breathlessness.
  • Baby younger than 3 months with cough and fever or poor feeding.
  • Sudden cough after choking, eating, playing with small objects, or possible foreign body aspiration.
  • Child is very drowsy, confused, floppy, unusually weak, or difficult to wake.
  • Signs of dehydration: very dry mouth, no tears, sunken eyes, or markedly reduced urine.
  • Persistent high fever, worsening fever, or fever with rash, severe pain, or neck stiffness.
  • Cough with blood, severe chest pain, or repeated vomiting.
  • Known asthma, heart disease, immune suppression, neuromuscular disease, or other significant medical condition with worsening cough.

6. When to book a non-urgent review

  • Cough lasting more than 2–3 weeks.
  • Repeated cough episodes affecting sleep, school, exercise, or feeding.
  • Night cough or exercise cough that keeps recurring.
  • Recurrent wheeze or repeated need for nebulisation/inhalers.
  • Poor weight gain, tiredness, or persistent reduced appetite.
  • Exposure to tuberculosis or persistent cough with weight loss or prolonged fever.
  • Parent concern even if no clear red flag is present.

7. Important facts and myth correction

  • Myth: Every cough needs antibiotics.
    Fact: Many childhood coughs are viral and antibiotics are not useful unless a bacterial infection is suspected or confirmed.
  • Myth: A strong cough syrup is the best treatment.
    Fact: Cough medicines may be unnecessary or unsafe in young children and should not be used without medical advice.
  • Myth: Nebulisation is needed for every cough.
    Fact: Nebulisation or inhalers are useful only for selected conditions such as wheeze/asthma and should be clinically guided.
  • Myth: Wet cough always means pneumonia.
    Fact: A wet-sounding cough can occur with common colds, but fever, fast breathing, chest indrawing, poor feeding, or worsening illness needs assessment.
  • Myth: Cough should stop immediately.
    Fact: Cough often takes time to settle even after a viral infection improves.

8. What to monitor at home

Observe Why it matters
Breathing effort Chest indrawing, grunting, or struggling to breathe needs urgent assessment.
Feeding and fluids Poor intake can lead to dehydration, especially in infants.
Urine output Markedly reduced urine may suggest dehydration.
Fever pattern Persistent or worsening fever may suggest a more significant infection.
Cough trigger Sudden onset after choking or eating may suggest aspiration.
Sleep and activity Reduced activity, lethargy, or poor sleep due to breathing difficulty needs review.
