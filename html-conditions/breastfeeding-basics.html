Breastfeeding Basics

Good attachment, good milk transfer, and confident early support.

Breastfeeding is learned together. Pain, poor latch, low urine output, jaundice, or poor weight gain need help early. Kind, practical lactation and paediatric support usually makes a big difference.

What are breastfeeding basics?

Breastfeeding basics include early feeding, comfortable positioning, deep latch, recognising swallowing, and knowing when to ask for help. Babies may feed often, especially in the early weeks.

Helpful signs

  • Baby opens the mouth wide and takes a large mouthful of breast, not just the nipple.
  • Mother may feel pulling, but persistent severe nipple pain needs support.
  • Baby has periods of rhythmic sucking and swallowing.
  • Wet nappies, stool pattern, alertness, jaundice checks, and weight review help confirm intake.

Common challenges

  • Incorrect latch, sleepy feeding, tongue-tie concern, maternal illness, delayed milk coming in, or separation after birth can affect feeding.
  • Premature or jaundiced babies may need extra feeding support and closer review.
  • Parents should not be blamed for feeding difficulty; early assessment is the priority.

Home support

  • Breastfeed responsively, including at night, unless your paediatrician has advised a specific plan.
  • Bring the baby close to the breast; avoid leaning the breast into the baby.
  • Check that the baby's nose is free and the chin is close to the breast.
  • Seek lactation or paediatric support for painful feeds, cracked nipples, engorgement, sleepy feeding, jaundice, or low urine output.
  • Mothers should drink to thirst, eat regular meals, and rest whenever possible.

Red flags / when to seek medical review

Seek urgent medical review if any of these occur:
  • Baby refuses feeds, is too sleepy to feed, or has a weak suck.
  • Very few wet nappies, dark urine, dry mouth, or other dehydration concern.
  • Fever or low temperature, lethargy, floppy baby, seizures, abnormal movements, inconsolable crying, or baby looks very unwell.
  • Fast breathing, grunting, chest indrawing, blue lips, pauses in breathing, choking, or repeated coughing during feeds.
  • Jaundice in the first 24 hours, deepening jaundice, poor feeding with jaundice, pale stools, or dark urine.
  • Repeated forceful vomiting, green vomit, abdominal distension, or parental concern.
  • Persistent painful latch, bleeding nipple, breast redness with fever, or severe maternal illness.

Important facts for parents

  • Exclusive breastfeeding for about the first 6 months is recommended where possible.
  • Small amounts of colostrum in the first days are valuable and normal.
  • A crying baby does not always mean low milk supply; review latch, urine, stool, weight, comfort, and jaundice.
  • Formula is not needed for every crying baby; use it when medically needed or by informed parental choice with safe preparation.
