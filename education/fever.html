Fever in Children: Age-Specific Parent Guide

By Dr Murali Gopal Published: May 2026 | Last updated: May 2026

Understanding Fever in Children

Fever is one of the most common concerns parents face. It's important to understand that fever itself is not an illness - it's a sign that your child's body is fighting an infection. Most fevers are caused by viral infections and resolve on their own.

Normal body temperature ranges: 36.5°C to 37.5°C (97.9°F to 99.5°F). A fever is typically defined as a temperature of 38°C (100.4°F) or higher when measured rectally in infants, or 38°C orally in older children.

This guide provides age-specific information. Click on your child's age group below to see recommendations tailored for them.

Newborns: 0-3 months

Why Newborns Need Special Care

Newborns have immature immune systems and cannot regulate their body temperature effectively. Any fever in this age group is considered a medical emergency and requires immediate evaluation.

Temperature Guidelines

Rectal temperature ≥ 38°C (100.4°F) = Medical emergency

Even a temperature of 38°C in a newborn requires urgent medical evaluation.

SEEK EMERGENCY CARE IMMEDIATELY

ANY fever in a newborn (0-3 months) is an emergency. Go to hospital or call emergency services.

  • Temperature ≥ 38°C rectally
  • Any signs of infection (rash, lethargy, difficulty feeding)
  • Unusual crying or behavior
  • Poor feeding or difficulty suckling

What to Do While Seeking Care

  • Do not wait. Seek medical care immediately - go to emergency department or call ambulance
  • Keep your baby warm with light clothing
  • Do NOT try to cool the baby down with ice baths
  • Have your baby's feeding history and recent care details ready

Key Points for Newborns

  • ANY fever in newborns is a medical emergency
  • Do not give fever-reducing medicines without doctor advice
  • Seek immediate medical evaluation
  • Avoid home remedies - professional care is essential
Infants: 3-12 months

Fever in Young Infants

Infants aged 3-12 months still have developing immune systems. While not all fevers are emergencies, any fever warrants prompt medical evaluation to rule out serious infections.

Temperature Guidelines

Rectal temperature ≥ 38°C (100.4°F) = Needs medical evaluation

Contact your doctor the same day or visit urgent care.

When to Seek Medical Care

  • Fever ≥ 38°C - contact doctor same day
  • Fever lasting more than 3 days
  • Fever with other symptoms (rash, vomiting, diarrhea)
  • Your baby seems unusually irritable or lethargic
  • Poor feeding or difficulty breastfeeding

SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE IF

  • Temperature > 39°C
  • Fever with unusual rash (especially if doesn't blanch to pressure)
  • Difficulty breathing or fast breathing
  • Baby is unusually drowsy or hard to wake
  • Refusing feeds completely
  • Any signs of dehydration (dry lips, fewer wet nappies)

Home Care While Waiting for Appointment

  • Monitor temperature: Take temperature every 2-3 hours
  • Keep hydrated: Offer frequent breastfeeds or bottle feeds
  • Dress lightly: Avoid heavy blankets (but keep baby warm)
  • Cool environment: Keep room at comfortable temperature
  • Do not give paracetamol or ibuprofen without doctor advice

Key Points for Infants

  • All fevers need medical evaluation - call your doctor
  • Do not give medicines without medical advice
  • Monitor feeding and wet nappies closely
  • Seek emergency care if baby seems unwell despite normal temperature
Toddlers: 1-3 years

Fever in Toddlers

Toddlers commonly develop fevers from viral infections. While most are not serious, it's important to monitor your child and seek care if the fever is high or lasts more than a few days.

Temperature Guidelines

Oral/axillary temperature 38-39°C: Monitor at home, contact doctor if concerned

Temperature > 39°C: Contact doctor same day or visit clinic

When to Contact Your Doctor

  • Fever > 39°C
  • Fever lasting more than 3-4 days
  • Fever with other symptoms (rash, cough, sore throat)
  • Your child seems unusually unwell or lethargic
  • Refusal to drink fluids
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhea

SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE IF

  • Fever with stiff neck or severe headache
  • Unusual rash that doesn't fade when pressed
  • Difficulty breathing
  • Persistent vomiting - cannot keep fluids down
  • Signs of dehydration (dry mouth, no tears when crying, fewer wet nappies)
  • Abdominal pain or unusual crying
  • Any convulsions or seizures

Home Care for Toddlers with Fever

  • Fluids: Offer water, diluted juice, or electrolyte drinks frequently
  • Light foods: Offer age-appropriate foods they enjoy
  • Clothing: Dress in light, breathable clothing
  • Temperature monitoring: Check every 4 hours or as needed
  • Cool compress: Gentle lukewarm sponging if very uncomfortable (not ice baths)
  • Medications: Only use paracetamol or ibuprofen as directed by your doctor

Febrile Seizures (Convulsions with Fever)

Some toddlers may have convulsions when fever rises suddenly. This is called a febrile seizure and is frightening but usually harmless.

  • If it happens: Keep your child safe (on their side), don't restrain them, note duration
  • Seek emergency care after the seizure stops
  • Tell doctor about the seizure so they can evaluate your child

Key Points for Toddlers

  • Most fevers in toddlers are from viral infections and resolve on their own
  • Keep your child comfortable and hydrated
  • Monitor for other symptoms that might indicate need for care
  • Use fever-reducing medicines only if recommended by doctor
  • Contact doctor if fever lasts > 3-4 days or if child seems very unwell
Preschool: 3-6 years

Fever in Preschool Children

By age 3-6, children's immune systems are more mature. Most fevers are from common viral infections (colds, flu) and are not serious. However, monitor for symptoms that suggest need for medical care.

Temperature Guidelines

Oral temperature 38-39°C: Usually manageable at home with comfort measures

Temperature > 39.5°C: Contact doctor if concerned or child seems very unwell

When to Contact Your Doctor

  • Fever lasting more than 4-5 days
  • Very high fever (> 40°C) that concerns you
  • Fever with other symptoms (severe cough, ear pain, sore throat)
  • Your child seems unusually unwell or lethargic
  • Refusal to drink or eat for extended periods
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhea

SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE IF

  • Stiff neck with fever and headache
  • Unusual rash that doesn't fade when pressed
  • Difficulty breathing or rapid breathing
  • Persistent, severe vomiting
  • Severe abdominal pain
  • Extreme lethargy - difficult to wake
  • Any signs of dehydration despite offering fluids

Home Care for Preschoolers with Fever

  • Hydration: Encourage water, diluted juices, soups, popsicles
  • Nutrition: Offer favorite (nutritious) foods they'll eat
  • Rest: Allow quiet activities, limit running around
  • Clothing: Dress lightly, avoid excessive bundling
  • Temperature check: Monitor morning and evening, or more if needed
  • Comfort measures: Lukewarm sponging if child is uncomfortable
  • Medicines: Use only on doctor's recommendation, at prescribed dose

When Fever Might Indicate Something Serious

While most fevers are harmless, these situations may indicate serious infection:

  • Fever with rash
  • Fever with extreme irritability or lethargy
  • Fever with unusual behavior or confusion
  • Fever with very high temperature that doesn't come down with rest

Key Points for Preschoolers

  • Fever is usually a sign of common infection that will resolve
  • Focus on keeping child comfortable and hydrated
  • Monitor for other symptoms that suggest need for doctor visit
  • Trust your instinct - if something seems wrong, seek medical advice
  • School/daycare: Most require fever-free for 24 hours before return
Older Children: 6+ years

Fever in School-Age and Older Children

Older children's immune systems are well-developed. Most fevers are from viral infections that resolve without treatment. Your child can often tell you how they're feeling, which helps guide care decisions.

Temperature Guidelines

Oral temperature 38-39°C: Usually manageable at home

Temperature > 39.5°C: Consider contacting doctor if very uncomfortable or other symptoms

When to Contact Your Doctor

  • Fever lasting more than 5-7 days
  • High fever (> 40°C) that concerns you despite comfort measures
  • Fever with severe symptoms (severe throat pain, ear pain, severe cough)
  • Child seems unusually unwell or lethargic
  • Refusal to drink or eat for extended periods
  • Persistent vomiting or diarrhea with fever

SEEK IMMEDIATE CARE IF

  • Stiff neck with fever and headache
  • Rash with fever (especially if doesn't fade when pressed)
  • Severe difficulty swallowing (unable to drink)
  • Difficulty breathing or fast breathing
  • Severe, persistent vomiting
  • Severe abdominal pain with fever
  • Extreme drowsiness or confusion
  • Any signs suggesting meningitis or serious infection

Home Care for School-Age Children with Fever

  • Hydration: Encourage water, juices, soups throughout the day
  • Nutrition: Offer balanced meals and snacks as tolerated
  • Rest: Allow adequate sleep and quiet activities
  • Activity: Light activity okay if child feels well enough; no strenuous exercise
  • Temperature monitoring: Check morning and evening
  • Medicines: Paracetamol or ibuprofen only if recommended by doctor
  • School/Sports: Child should be fever-free for 24 hours before returning

Communicating with Your Child

  • Ask how they're feeling (headache, sore throat, body aches)
  • Listen to their concerns
  • Reassure them that fever helps fight infection
  • Encourage fluids in a positive way ("Let's see how much water you can drink")

Key Points for Older Children

  • Most fevers are viral and resolve without treatment in 3-5 days
  • Fever itself is not dangerous - it helps fight infection
  • Focus on comfort and hydration
  • Trust your judgment - you know your child best
  • Contact doctor if fever lasts > 5-7 days or with concerning symptoms
  • School policy: Usually requires 24-hour fever-free period before return

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: How should I take my child's temperature?

For age 0-3 months: Rectal thermometer (most accurate). Place baby on their back, insert thermometer gently into rectum, hold for 1 minute.

For age 3 months to 4 years: Rectal or axillary (armpit). Axillary is easier but slightly less accurate.

For age 4+ years: Oral (under tongue) is most accurate. Axillary or temporal scan also acceptable.

Q: Should I treat every fever?

No. Fever is your child's body fighting infection. If your child is comfortable, hydrated, and playing normally, you don't need to give fever-reducing medicines. Only use medicines if your doctor recommends them.

Q: Is a high fever dangerous?

Most fevers below 40-41°C are not dangerous, even if they seem high. The fever itself doesn't cause harm. However, very high fevers might cause discomfort. Contact your doctor if concerned.

Q: Can fever cause brain damage?

No. Fever from infection does not cause brain damage. Fevers would need to exceed 42-43°C to cause problems, which is rare. This misconception causes unnecessary worry.

Q: When should my child return to school?

Most schools require children to be fever-free (without fever-reducing medicines) for 24 hours before returning. Check your school's specific policy.

Q: What about "fever phobia"?

Many parents worry excessively about fever. Remember: fever is a sign of infection, not an illness itself. A fever shows your child's immune system is working! Trust your instincts and call your doctor with concerns.
